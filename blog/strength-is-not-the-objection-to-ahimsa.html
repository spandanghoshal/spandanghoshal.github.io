Strength Is Not the Objection to Ahiṃsā

A reply to the Hobbesian reading of my last essay — that the world is a jungle, and that Krṣṇa himself fought by deceit rather than harm-minimisation. The objection mistakes the boundary condition for the objective.

The most serious response to my last essay did not dispute the linear programme. It disputed the premise that minimising harm is the right thing to optimise at all. The argument, roughly: the same verse I quoted — jīvo jīvasya jīvanam, one life is the food of another — describes a jungle, not a sástra. The strong prey on the weak as a law of nature, and any civilisation that optimises for gentleness over strength ends up subjugated by one that does not. Even Krṣṇa, the argument goes, lied, schemed, and manipulated the field at Kurukṣetra — that is how battles are actually won, not by minimising harm. So why build a model whose objective function is softness?

This deserves a real answer, not a dismissal. And the answer is that the objection has smuggled in an extra assumption that the original model never made.

The constraint was never “never use force”

Go back to the programme. The objective was \(\min \sum h_i x_i\) subject to the body's nutritional needs being met. Nothing in that statement says subject to: never be capable of violence. It says nothing about geopolitics, self-defence, or the strength required to not be somebody else's prey. Those are different decision variables in a different, much larger optimisation — the one that governs how a person or a civilisation survives among other agents who may not share its objective. Conflating the two programmes is the entire error. A king maintaining an army and a household choosing what to eat for dinner are not solving the same problem, and a model built for the second was never claiming to settle the first.

This is, in fact, exactly how the śāstra itself partitions the question. Dharma is not one rule for every varṇa; it is a feasible region that depends on one's position in the larger system. A kṣatriya's dharma includes the disciplined use of force in defence of the innocent — that is not a violation of ahiṃsā, it is ahiṃsā applied at a different boundary, where the harm of inaction (a kingdom overrun, the weak left undefended) exceeds the harm of action. The Bhagavad-gītā is, after all, addressed to a warrior on a battlefield, not a renunciate in a forest. Arjuna is told to fight. The text that contains the most complete statement of non-attachment to outcome is delivered as the justification for picking up a bow.

What Krṣṇa's strategy actually optimised

So the Kurukṣetra point cuts the opposite way from how it was raised. Krṣṇa's counsel — concealing Arjuna's identity to draw out Karna's armour, advising the strike that ends Jayadratha before sunset, all of it — was deployed in service of a specific, bounded objective: that adharma should not inherit the earth. It was not deployed as a general endorsement of deceit as a way of life, and the text is careful to show the cost: the Gītā's battlefield is framed throughout as a tragedy, not a triumph, fought because every gentler constraint had already failed — thirteen years of exile, repeated attempts at peace, Krṣṇa's own embassy to Hastināpura asking for five villages in lieu of a kingdom. Force was the solution of last resort to a programme whose constraints had been exhausted, not the default objective. That is not evidence against optimising for minimal harm; it is evidence that the harm-minimising solution, correctly computed over the entire situation including Duryodhana's refusal to yield anything, was war — and even then, a war fought with the maximum cunning available, precisely to shorten it and reduce the total casualties on both sides relative to a longer, less decisive campaign.

Strategic intelligence in service of minimising total harm is not a refutation of harm-minimisation. It is harm-minimisation done competently, the way a surgeon's scalpel is not a refutation of the Hippocratic oath.

Back to the actual question: whose feasible region is this?

Strip away the Kurukṣetra framing and the original objection reduces to something more modest and more answerable: given that time, place, and circumstance genuinely change the constraint set, what does the constraint set look like for most people, most of the time, in the world as it actually is today? That is the right question, and it is an empirical one, not a metaphysical one.

There are real exceptions, and the śāstra has always known this. A household on Arctic tundra, ten months of snow, no arable soil — the feasible region for that population genuinely excludes a plant-only diet, and no amount of moral preference changes the constraint \(\sum n_{ij} x_i \ge N_j\) for a body that cannot synthesise certain nutrients from what the land will grow there. That is a spatial argument, and I conceded it the first time; I concede it again.

But set the spatial exception aside and ask about the much larger population for whom it does not apply: people with reasonable access to grains, legumes, dairy, and a varied agricultural base — which today is most of the literate, urban, and even rural world outside the genuine extremes. For that population the world we actually live in is, by any reasonable historical comparison, unusually peaceful. Large-scale, civilisation-threatening war is, for most people reading this, not the operative constraint on their dinner the way it might have been for a population under active siege. And once existential conflict drops out of a population's actual feasible region, the calculus changes: the marginal value of the warrior's exception shrinks, and the original harm-minimisation programme — the one about food, not about armies — reasserts itself as the binding one.

Food, guṇa, and the thing the “jungle” argument gets backwards

There is a further point the strength argument misses entirely, and it is worth stating plainly because it inverts the objection. The śāstra's claim is not only that meat-eating is high on the harm coefficient \(h_i\); it is that the guṇa of one's food feeds back into the mind that does the optimising in the first place. Food taken in the modes of passion and ignorance (rājasic and tāmasic food) is repeatedly associated, across the literature and in a fair amount of contemporary research on diet and affect, with exactly the propensities — aggression, restlessness, identification with the body, appetite for domination — that produce the jungle the objection takes as a brute, unalterable fact of nature. If most wars trace back to ego, to a strong identification with body, nation, and the greed for power or wealth, then a food regime that measurably calms those propensities is not a naive irrelevance to the question of strength. It is upstream of it. The objection assumes the jungle is exogenous — a fixed fact the optimiser must simply accommodate. The śāstric claim is that the jungle is, in part, endogenous: partly a function of what a civilisation eats, and therefore a variable inside the very optimisation the objection wants to place outside it.

None of this requires denying that strength and capability matter, or that a population unable to defend itself is at the mercy of one that can. It only requires noticing that strength and ahiṃsā are not opposed terms in the same equation. Sportspeople, bodybuilders, and combat athletes who train and compete on a vegetarian diet are not a marginal curiosity; they are a standing existence proof that capability and the harm-minimising diet are not in tension for the overwhelming majority of bodies and circumstances. The exception is real but narrow — genuine ecological necessity, genuine illness, genuine siege. For everyone else, the binding constraint was never the jungle. It was appetite mistaking itself for necessity.

What I am and am not claiming

I am not claiming that force has no place, that Krṣṇa was wrong to counsel as He did, or that every person's constraint set looks like mine. I am claiming that the existence of a kṣatriya's dharma does not refute a vaiśya's or a brahmacāri's, and that the jungle verse from the Bhāgavatam was never an injunction — it was a description of the unavoidable cost of living, offered as the premise for a discipline of minimising that cost, not as licence to stop minimising it the moment one feels capable of doing otherwise. The strong are not exempted from the optimisation. They are simply better positioned to solve it well.
