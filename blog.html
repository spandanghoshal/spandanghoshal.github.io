Spirituality in the Age of AI

November 14, 2025 ¬∑ Reflections

Each week the news cycle heralds a new benchmark: models that compose symphonies, write proofs, or automate decisions that once required years of training. These advances are exhilarating, yet they also expose a quiet truth. If intelligence can be replicated in silicon, then intelligence alone cannot be the foundation of our worth. The prestige attached to material productivity becomes fragile the moment an algorithm performs the same task faster and cheaper.

This is why spiritual practice feels more relevant than ever. Through japa, study of the Gƒ´tƒÅ, and time in satsanga, I receive a reminder that consciousness is not a competition. The goal is not to outrun machines but to realign with the qualities that machines cannot imitate: compassion, surrender, and the capacity to be present with another being's suffering. When I begin my workday after meditation, the rush to prove myself to an invisible marketplace subsides. What remains is gratitude for the chance to serve, whether by writing code, teaching a concept, or listening to a friend.

Artificial intelligence may erode the scarcity of material achievement, but it also frees us to ask deeper questions. What is the purpose of my intellect? How do I use it to cultivate seva rather than status? Spiritual traditions offer practices that keep these questions alive. They guide us to anchor identity in the eternal, not in metrics that can be optimized away. In that anchoring lies a joy that no algorithm can synthesize.
